How does targeted therapy differ from chemotherapy?

Targeted therapy differs from chemotherapy in its approach to treating cancer. While chemotherapy is a systemic treatment that affects rapidly dividing cells throughout the body, targeted therapy specifically targets molecular and genetic changes within cancer cells. This type of therapy is designed to interfere with particular molecules involved in cancer cell growth and survival. Targeted therapies can include monoclonal antibodies, which bind to specific proteins on cancer cells, and small molecule inhibitors, which block the action of enzymes or other proteins critical for cancer cell function. Because targeted therapies focus on specific abnormalities in cancer cells, they often have a different side effect profile compared to chemotherapy. Targeted therapies are generally designed to be more selective, which can lead to fewer side effects and potentially more effective treatment outcomes for certain types of cancer.

Hormone therapy is used to treat cancers that are influenced by hormone levels, such as certain types of breast and prostate cancer. This treatment works by altering the body's hormone levels or blocking hormone receptors on cancer cells, which can slow or stop the growth of tumors that depend on these hormones. In breast cancer, hormone therapy might involve medications that reduce estrogen levels or block estrogen receptors on cancer cells. For prostate cancer, hormone therapy often includes medications that lower testosterone levels or block its effects. Hormone therapy can be employed as a primary treatment, to shrink tumors before surgery, or to reduce the risk of recurrence after initial treatment.
